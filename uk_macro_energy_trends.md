    <h3>Engineering Study: The Real Peak Challenge of Electrifying Heat and Transport</h3>
    <p>While the historical ONS data above tracks the decline of coal and the plateau of gas, the transition to full electrification radically alters both <b>Primary Energy Demand</b> and <b>Peak Grid Load</b>.</p>
    <ul>
        <li>
            <b>The Efficiency Dividend:</b> Electrification can reduce the UK's total primary energy demand by up to 70%. 
            Heat pumps typically operate at 300% to 450% efficiency (SCOP 3.0 to 4.5), requiring roughly 3.5 kW 
            of electrical input to deliver approximately 11-14 kW of thermal output. Similarly, electric vehicles 
            convert ~70% of energy to motion, compared to roughly 20-25% for internal combustion engines.
        </li>
        <li>
            <b>The Peak Load Stress Test:</b> A theoretical scenario where 28 million homes draw 3.5 kW for heat 
            pumps simultaneously, alongside 20 million EVs charging at 7.4 kW, would create an unmanaged 246 GW 
            peak demand spike. The current UK grid is designed to meet a winter peak of approximately 48.3 GW.
        </li>
        <li>
            <b>Managed Diversity:</b> Accounting for load diversity (approx. 1.7 kW per home) and smart EV charging, 
            the realistic managed peak load ceiling is projected between 90 and 100 GW.
        </li>
        <li>
            <b>Infrastructure Resilience:</b> To prevent localized failures (such as the 2025 Heathrow Airport 
            substation blackout), grid operators must rigorously design for coincident peaks, implementing 
            advanced protection coordination, relay settings, and insulation coordination.
        </li>
    </ul>
