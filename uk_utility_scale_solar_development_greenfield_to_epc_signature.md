# Developing a Utility-Scale Solar Farm in the UK: From Greenfield to EPC Signature

## Executive Summary

A typical UK utility-scale solar farm — say a 49.9 MW project under the Town and Country Planning Act, or a 100 MW+ Nationally Significant Infrastructure Project (NSIP) under the Development Consent Order regime — takes roughly 4–7 years from greenfield site identification to EPC contract signature and absorbs **£1.5–4.5 million** of at-risk development capital (≈ £30,000–£90,000 per MW) before financial close. Land control, planning consent, an accepted grid connection offer, and an offtake/route-to-market arrangement are the four non-negotiable gates that must be in place before an EPC contract can be signed and project finance drawn.

The journey is now being reshaped by three structural reforms that any developer must internalise:

* **NSIP threshold raised from 50 MW to 100 MW** (*Infrastructure Planning (Onshore Wind and Solar Generation) Order 2025*), in force from 31 December 2025 with a one-year transitional window — moving the planning "dead zone" up from 49.9 MW to ~99.9 MW.
* **TMO4+ Connections Reform** (Ofgem-approved 15 April 2025; "live" from 10 June 2025): the historic "first come, first served" queue is replaced with a "first ready, first connected" gated process administered by NESO (the National Energy System Operator, which took over from National Grid ESO in October 2024). Around 482 GW of the existing 596 GW queue is expected to drop to Gate 1 or be terminated, with the new Gate 2 queue sized to roughly 296 GW aligned to Clean Power 2030/2035 capacities.
* **Clean Power 2030 Action Plan** (December 2024) targets 45–57 GW of solar by 2030 (from ~18.1 GW installed in March 2025), with mandatory 10% Biodiversity Net Gain (BNG) applying since February 2024 and AR7a in early 2026 awarding a record 4.9 GW of solar at a strike price of £65/MWh (2024 prices).

Industry analysts now most-cited in UK solar development decisions include Solar Energy UK, RenewableUK, Cornwall Insight (PPA tracking and route-to-market revenue forecasting), LCP Delta (CfD bid strategy and wholesale market modelling), Pexapark and LevelTen (European PPA price indices), and Aurora Energy Research (Clean Power 2030 stress-testing). DESNZ commissioned Arup's July 2025 *Renewable Energy Generation Cost and Technical Assumptions 2024* study as the official cost baseline for AR7 and AR8.

The report below walks through each milestone of the development journey, with current UK-specific figures, gatekeeping requirements, and common failure points.

## 1. Site Identification and Land Control

### How developers find sites

The three primary screening criteria are:

* **Grid proximity:** Sites within roughly 1–10 miles (and ideally <1 mile) of a 33 kV, 66 kV, 132 kV or 400 kV connection point. Conrad Energy's George Hall describes "less than 10 miles from an electricity substation" as the principal critical test. Above ~50 MW, a transmission (132 kV/400 kV) connection via NESO is needed; below ~50 MW, a distribution (DNO) connection at 33 kV or 132 kV is typical.
* **Solar irradiance:** Irradiance in southern England (Kent, Wiltshire, Hampshire, Cornwall, Suffolk) is materially higher than in northern England and Scotland, and lease rents reflect this — Savills and Farmers Weekly both note "lower rents in the north of the country where solar radiation levels are lower."
* **Land type and constraints:** Open, gently sloping (preferably south-facing) land, away from designated landscapes (National Parks, AONBs/National Landscapes), heritage assets, ancient woodland, SSSIs, flood zones 2/3, the Green Belt (now requiring "very special circumstances" for sub-100 MW projects under the December 2024 NPPF revision), and major aviation safeguarding zones.

### Agricultural Land Classification (ALC) and Best and Most Versatile (BMV) land

Land is graded 1 (excellent) to 5 (very poor). Grades 1, 2 and 3a constitute Best and Most Versatile (BMV) agricultural land. National Policy Statement EN-3 (updated in 2024) does not prohibit solar on BMV land, but developers must demonstrate that poorer-quality land cannot accommodate the project. Projects affecting more than 20 hectares of BMV land must consult Natural England.

In practice, most developers and landowners target Grade 3b, 4 and 5 land (RWE, for instance, explicitly states "preferably we are looking for grade 3 land or worse"). The December 2024 NPPF revision removed a previous provision allowing LPAs to refuse solar on food-security grounds, but local sensitivity remains acute and a robust site-specific ALC survey is now near-universal. Mis-classification of land at planning stage — or relying on the 1980s/1990s national ALC maps rather than a site-specific ALC survey — is one of the most common reasons for refusal.

### Site size: MW-per-acre rules of thumb

Industry rule-of-thumb in the UK is **2–2.5 acres per MWp** (≈ 0.8–1 ha/MWp), with modern bifacial single-axis tracker designs at the lower end. ILOS Energy's published case-study scaling is "1 MW per 2.4 acres" — so a 50 MW project typically takes ~120 acres (~48 ha), a 100 MW project ~250 acres (~100 ha) and the largest UK NSIPs (e.g. the 500 MW Sunnica, 600 MW Cottam, 400 MW Longfield, 500 MW Heckington Fen) extend to 1,000+ ha. As of September 2024, UK ground-mount solar covered ~21,200 hectares — about 0.1% of UK land area.

### Option agreements, leases and Heads of Terms

A developer almost always secures land through a two-stage structure:

| Instrument | Typical Duration | Typical Financial Terms (2025/26) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Exclusivity / Heads of Terms** | 1–3 months | Often nil or nominal (£1k–£10k) |
| **Option Agreement** | 2–5 years (typically 3 years + 1–2 year extensions) | Option fee £100–£500 per acre per year, sometimes paid as a lump sum at signing; developer covers all planning/grid/legal costs |
| **Lease** *(triggered on planning consent)* | 25–40 years, sometimes 50 years; renewal options common | £850–£1,350 per acre per year (Savills, Farmers Weekly, SolarGridCheck 2026), indexed to RPI/CPI. Alternative: 5–6% of turnover ("top-up" mechanism), worth ~£1,400–£1,500/acre at current power prices on a good site |

A decommissioning bond of £15,000–£25,000 per MW is now standard market practice, sometimes held by the developer's funder or via an on-balance-sheet provision. Section 106 agreements with the LPA frequently include a landscape and ecology management plan obligation.

Heads of Terms are usually 8–15 pages and cover: option period and extension fees; rent (acreage vs turnover hybrid); permitted use; insolvency / step-in for lenders; sub-letting and assignment to a SPV at financial close; reinstatement and decommissioning bond; tenancy security excluded from Landlord & Tenant Act 1954 Part II; and tax indemnities (particularly Agricultural Property Relief and Business Property Relief).

### Key documents and deliverables at this stage

* Site shortlist memo
* Constraints map (GIS overlay of grid, irradiance, ALC, designations, flood, heritage, ecology)
* Heads of Terms
* Option Agreement
* Agreed form of Lease
* Landowner consultation log

### Common failure points at this stage

* Landlord pulling out before option exercise (no security of tenure).
* "Clustering" disputes where multiple developers chase the same grid node and overlapping landholdings.
* Failure to control land for cable corridor, substation footprint and BNG off-site units — increasingly a problem as off-site BNG can require additional 15–30% land area.
* The High Court has recently confirmed (2024) that developing a project adjacent to an existing solar farm may be deemed an "extension" — pushing combined capacity over the NSIP threshold and forcing the project into the DCO regime ("Beware the slip into NSIP").

## 2. Grid Connection

### Institutional framework

* **NESO (National Energy System Operator)** — operational since October 2024, replacing National Grid ESO. NESO is publicly owned and reports to DESNZ/Ofgem. It runs the transmission queue and the new Gate 1/Gate 2 process.
* **Transmission Owners (TOs)** — National Grid Electricity Transmission (NGET) in England & Wales, SP Transmission (Scottish Power) in southern Scotland, SSEN Transmission in northern Scotland.
* **Distribution Network Operators (DNOs)** — UK Power Networks, Northern Powergrid, Electricity North West, SP Distribution, SSEN Distribution, National Grid Electricity Distribution (formerly WPD).
* **Ofgem** regulates network charges and approves connection code modifications (CMPs).

### TNUoS, BSUoS and DUoS — the charging framework

Generators pay **Transmission Network Use of System (TNUoS)** charges set by NESO and administered by NGET, varying by zone — historically strongly negative (a credit) in southern England and strongly positive (a cost) in northern Scotland, encouraging generation to locate near demand. Reforms under TCR (Targeted Charging Review) and now the Strategic Spatial Energy Plan (SSEP) work are flattening these signals. **Balancing Services Use of System (BSUoS)** has, since April 2023, been paid by suppliers only (final demand), eliminating one major historical cost for generators. Distribution-connected projects also pay (or receive) **Distribution Use of System (DUoS)** charges, with embedded benefits eroded by Targeted Charging Review changes phased in from 2018.

### TMO4+ Connections Reform — the most important change for solar developers

Ofgem approved the TMO4+ ("First Ready, First Connected") reform package on 15 April 2025, and the new regime went live on 10 June 2025. Key features:

* **Two gates:**
* **Gate 1:** indicative offer only — no confirmed connection date, no firm queue position, no connection point. For projects that do not meet readiness or strategic-alignment criteria.
* **Gate 2:** firm offer with confirmed connection date, point and queue position. Requires that the project be "ready" (secured land rights, sufficiently advanced planning) and "needed" (aligned with Clean Power 2030 Action Plan capacity envelopes for the relevant technology, zone and decade).


* **Gate 2 to Whole Queue (G2TWQ) process:** all existing offer-holders (~596 GW) had to re-evidence by 26 August 2025 (extended from 29 July). NESO estimated ~482 GW will be pushed to Gate 1 or terminated, leaving a firm Gate 2 queue of ~296 GW (including built capacity) aligned with CPAP capacities to 2035.
* **Revised offer timeline (NESO update, December 2025):** Gate 2 Phase 1 transmission/large-embedded offers between mid-May 2026 and mid-September 2026; Gate 2 Phase 1 distribution offers between early July 2026 and mid-November 2026; Phase 2 transmission offers between early September 2026 and mid-January 2027; Phase 2 distribution between mid-October 2026 and mid-March 2027. This is a 4–5 month delay versus NESO's original timetable.
* **Annual application windows thereafter (at minimum)** — the next window after the August 2025 closure is not expected before mid-2026, frustrating developers who landed in Gate 1.
* **Connections Action Plan (CAP)** — the joint Ofgem/DESNZ document published in late 2023, which sets the policy framework for TMO4+ and the ENA's three-point plan on queue management.

### Readiness criteria for Gate 2 (solar)

Broadly:

1. Demonstrable land rights — option, lease or freehold over the project site
2. Submitted planning application or planning permission already granted, depending on project size
3. Committed funding for connection-stage securities
4. Red-line boundary defined
5. For transmission-connected projects, a Transmission Impact Assessment commenced.

### Connection costs, fees and securities

| Item | Typical figure (2025/26) |
| --- | --- |
| **Initial connection application fee** (DNO / NESO) | £5,000–£50,000 depending on size |
| **Grid studies** (System Impact Study, Connection Impact Assessment, ER P28/G99) | £30,000–£200,000 |
| **TNUoS** (Transmission Network Use of System) — generators | Zone-dependent (can be a credit in southern England, a charge in northern Scotland). Highly material for project IRR. |
| **Connection construction cost** (developer-funded works) | Typically £150,000–£500,000 per MW for a 33 kV DNO connection; up to £1–3 million per MW for transmission-connected projects requiring deep reinforcement. |
| **Securities** | Liability cap securities (bank/parent guarantees) typically ~£17,000–£25,000 per MW per year of remaining works, indexed; plus the new Progression Commitment Fee (PCF) under CMP448. |
| **Typical end-to-end signed offer to energisation** | Historically 4–7 years; under TMO4+ for Gate 2 projects, NESO is targeting "as soon as 2027" for Phase 1. |

### Transmission-connected vs distribution-connected

* **Transmission-connected** (typically > ~50 MW, or any project requiring direct entry to the 132 kV+ network): NESO is the offer-issuing party; offers are termed BEGA/BELLA/CIA. Subject to TNUoS, Balancing Mechanism participation, BSC accession.
* **Distribution-connected** ("embedded"): DNO issues the offer, with NESO providing the final Gate 2 determination above the relevant Transmission Impact Assessment (TIA) threshold (1 MW at most GSPs, though for some heavily-loaded GSPs NESO has agreed higher thresholds under CMP446). Embedded benefits have been largely eroded by reforms since 2018 but Triad-replacement and DUoS exposure remain.

### Key documents and deliverables at this stage

* Application form (CP47/CP14/CP15 family)
* Construction Agreement (BCA, MCUSA, BELLA, BEGA)
* System Impact Study / Connection Impact Assessment
* Design package to ER G99 standard
* Readiness evidence pack (lender's expected diligence pack)
* Securities/bank guarantees in place

### Common failure points

* Being assigned Gate 1 rather than Gate 2 because planning is not yet submitted or land rights are insufficient — effectively a multi-year setback.
* Strategic mis-alignment: the Clean Power 2030 zonal capacities cap how much new solar can be accommodated regionally — projects in already-saturated regions face the highest risk.
* Curtailment risk: distribution networks increasingly require "active network management" (ANM) flexible/non-firm connections, which can curtail output 5–25% in early years.
* NESO delays: industry confidence in the December 2025 revised timetable is low; Ofgem in late 2025 was publicly "frustrated" with delays affecting 62% of Gate 2 project connections.

## 3. Planning and Consenting

### England — Town and Country Planning Act 1990 (≤ 100 MW from 31 December 2025)

From the implementation date of the *Infrastructure Planning (Onshore Wind and Solar Generation) Order 2025* on 31 December 2025, projects with a generating capacity below 100 MW in England are determined by the Local Planning Authority (LPA). A one-year transitional window allows 50–100 MW projects already in pre-application to choose between the LPA route and the NSIP route until end-2026.

**Typical TCPA timeline and cost (2025/26):**

| Stage | Duration | Cost |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Pre-application discussion with LPA, Statement of Community Involvement** | 3–6 months | £20,000–£80,000 |
| **EIA Screening & Scoping** (Environmental Impact Assessment Regs 2017) | 2–3 months | £15,000–£40,000 |
| **Environmental Statement preparation** (full suite of surveys) | 6–12 months | £150,000–£500,000 |
| **Application validation** (LPA fee scales with site area) | 2–4 weeks | LPA application fee typically £30,000–£60,000 |
| **Statutory determination** ("8 weeks" / "16 weeks for EIA development" in practice) | 8–13 weeks if non-EIA; 6–18 months commonly with PPAs | LPA PPA fees £20,000–£100,000 |
| **Section 106 negotiation** | 1–4 months | Legal fees £25,000–£75,000 |

Approval rate for solar farm applications at LPA level is approximately 66%. Refusals can be appealed to the Planning Inspectorate (PINS) — appeal success rates for solar have historically been high (>50%) but add 9–18 months and £100,000–£300,000 in legal/consultant fees.

### England — NSIP / Development Consent Order (≥ 100 MW from 31 December 2025)

For solar above 100 MW in England (or above 350 MW in Wales), the project is consented as a Nationally Significant Infrastructure Project under the Planning Act 2008. The Secretary of State for Energy Security and Net Zero (DESNZ) is the decision-maker; the Planning Inspectorate (PINS) examines.

**The statutory DCO timetable is roughly:**

| Stage | Statutory minimum | Practical reality |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Pre-application** (scoping, statutory consultation) | No statutory minimum | Typically 18–30 months |
| **Acceptance** | 28 days | 28 days |
| **Pre-examination** | ~3 months | ~3 months |
| **Examination** | 6 months | 6 months |
| **Recommendation** (PINS to SoS) | 3 months | 3 months |
| **Decision** (SoS) | 3 months | 3 months (often slips) |
| **Legal challenge window** | 6 weeks | Now reduced under Banner Review reforms |

**Total DCO timeline:** typically 3.5–5 years from project inception to consent. DCO costs for a large solar farm are routinely £3–8 million.

### Wales — Developments of National Significance (DNS)

Projects 10–350 MW in Wales follow the Developments of National Significance (DNS) route, decided by Welsh Ministers via Planning and Environment Decisions Wales (PEDW). DNS examinations are statutorily faster than DCOs (typically ~36 weeks from acceptance to recommendation).

### Scotland — Section 36 of the Electricity Act 1989

Onshore generating stations over 50 MW in Scotland require Section 36 consent from Scottish Ministers via the Energy Consents Unit (ECU). Below 50 MW the local planning authority decides. Typical S36 timeline is 2–3 years including the EIA report.

### Northern Ireland

Projects under 5 MW are LPA-determined; 5 MW and above are "major developments" decided by the Department for Infrastructure (12-week notice required).

### Common reasons UK solar planning applications fail

* BMV land use without robust justification.
* Landscape and visual impact — particularly proximity to National Landscapes (AONBs).
* Heritage impact — setting of listed buildings, conservation areas, scheduled monuments.
* Cumulative impact with neighbouring solar/BESS schemes.
* Highway access during construction (HGV movements).
* Glint and glare affecting residences, aviation or transport.
* Inadequate BNG / ecological mitigation under the post-2024 regime.
* Insufficient pre-application consultation (especially fatal in DCO acceptance).

## 5. Design and Engineering

### Preliminary design and FEED

* **Concept / Pre-feasibility design (Year 1):** irradiance modelling, coarse layout to support land option and grid application. Cost: £15,000–£40,000.
* **Planning-stage design:** indicative layout, parameters (Rochdale Envelope), Single Line Diagrams to ER G99 level.
* **FEED / Detailed design / IFC (Year 3–5):** bankable energy yield report (P50/P90/P99), final layout, geotechnical-driven foundation design, protection coordination studies, SCADA architecture. FEED cost: £75,000–£300,000.

### Technology choices (2025/26 market reality)

* **Modules:** bifacial monocrystalline (typically 580–620 W). Module cost has fallen sharply: forecast £200,000/MW for 2030 versus £270,000/MW in 2019.
* **Mounting:** single-axis trackers command ~£0.65–0.85/Wp EPC pricing and account for 55–60% of new utility-scale projects. Fixed-tilt remains common for smaller sites at £0.55–0.75/Wp.
* **DC:AC ratio:** now routinely 1.3–1.5:1 ("overplanting").
* **Inverters:** central inverters for 100 MW+; string inverters increasingly favoured below ~50 MW.

## 6. Offtake and Route to Market

### Contracts for Difference — the dominant subsidised route

The Government's CfD scheme provides a 15-year (now 20-year from AR7) two-way price guarantee.

| Round | Year | Solar Capacity Awarded | Solar Strike Price (2024 real) | Contract Length |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **AR4** | 2022 | 2.2 GW (67 projects) | £64.09/MWh | 15 years |
| **AR5** | 2023 | 1.9 GW (56 projects) | £65.49/MWh | 15 years |
| **AR6** | Sept 2024 | 3.3 GW (93 projects) | £72.92/MWh | 15 years |
| **AR7a** | Feb 2026 | 4.9 GW (155 projects) | £65.23/MWh | 20 years |

### Corporate PPAs (CPPAs)

* **Tenor:** typically 10–15 years.
* **Pricing:** UK utility-scale solar CPPAs are currently quoted in the £55–£80/MWh range as-generated, narrowing the merchant-vs-CfD trade-off.
* **Counterparties:** traditional utilities, data centres, tech corporates (Amazon, Microsoft, Google, Meta), and supermarkets.

### Merchant exposure and route-to-market

Most UK utility-scale solar projects financed today have a blended revenue stack: e.g. CfD on 70–80% of output + merchant tail; or PPA for 10 years + merchant. The "captured price" is now ~85% of baseload due to cannibalisation.

### Co-location with battery storage

Co-location is now the default design assumption for new build to capture curtailment, capacity-market revenues, and frequency-response. Co-location attracts EPC pricing premia of ~£0.05–0.10/Wp for shared infrastructure.

## 7. Financing Milestones

### Development capital

Development capital (the "at-risk" pre-FID spend) is borne entirely by the developer's equity.

| Stage | Cumulative spend (£/MW) | Cumulative absolute spend (50 MW) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Origination (site identification, option signing)** | £1,000–£3,000 | £50,000–£150,000 |
| **Grid application accepted (pre-Gate 2 evidence)** | £8,000–£15,000 | £400,000–£750,000 |
| **EIA / planning application submitted** | £15,000–£35,000 | £750,000–£1.75m |
| **Planning consent granted** | £25,000–£55,000 | £1.25m–£2.75m |
| **Gate 2 grid offer accepted, securities lodged** | £40,000–£70,000 | £2.0m–£3.5m |
| **CfD won OR PPA term sheet signed** | £50,000–£80,000 | £2.5m–£4.0m |
| **EPC contract signed / Financial Close** | £60,000–£100,000 | £3.0m–£5.0m |

### Total project capex at financial close

EPC alone is now £550,000–£850,000/MW; total project capex is therefore in the **£800,000–£1.1m/MW** range for typical UK projects in 2025/26. A 50 MW project implies roughly £40–55 million total capex.

### Project finance structure

* **Capital structure:** 70:30 to 75:25 non-recourse senior debt:equity with a CfD; 60:40 to 65:35 for fully merchant or short-PPA projects.
* **Senior debt tenor:** 15–18 years amortising, often "soft mini-perm" with refinancing assumed at year 5–7.

## 8. EPC Contract

### Tendering and selection

Most utility-scale UK solar projects run a competitive two- or three-stage EPC tender (EOI/RFI -> RFP -> Negotiation/Award).

### EPC pricing (UK, 2025/26)

| Configuration | EPC price (£/Wp) | Implied £/MW (DC) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **50 MW fixed-tilt, full wrap, no BESS** | £0.55–0.75/Wp | £550,000–£750,000/MW |
| **50 MW single-axis tracker, full wrap, no BESS** | £0.65–0.85/Wp | £650,000–£850,000/MW |
| **100 MW+ tracker with co-located 2-hour BESS** | £0.85–1.20/Wp (combined) | Variable |

### Risk allocation and gatekeeping

A bankable EPC for UK solar will typically contain:

* Lump-sum, date-certain, fully-wrapped scope.
* Performance Liquidated Damages (delay LDs ~0.1–0.2% of contract value per day, capped at 10–15%).
* Total liability cap typically 100% of contract value.

### What must be in place before EPC signature

1. **Land secured** — option exercised into a registered long-term lease.
2. **Planning consent** — non-judicially-reviewable.
3. **Accepted grid connection offer** with confirmed Gate 2 status under TMO4+.
4. **Offtake / route to market** — either an executed CfD, executed CPPA, or acceptable merchant strategy.
5. **Insurance package** in place.
6. **Equity funded** and senior debt facility agreements signed.
7. **EPC contractor selected**, with parent company guarantee and performance bond.

## 9. REPD-Derived UK Solar Project Data

The Renewable Energy Planning Database (REPD) is the canonical record of UK renewables projects above 150 kW:

* **Typical project size:** until 2024 the modal utility-scale UK solar project was 49.9 MW. Above this, the pipeline jumps to NSIP-scale clusters around 350–600 MW. The AR7a results show an average winning solar project size of just 31.6 MW.
* **Approval rates at LPA level:** ~66% of solar farm applications are approved on first determination.
* **Time from application to consent:** typically 12–18 months.
* **Time from consent to construction start:** typically 18–36 months.
* **Attrition between development stages:**
* Sites identified → option signed: ~40–50%
* Option signed → grid offer accepted: ~50–70%
* Grid offer → planning submitted: ~70–85%
* Planning submitted → planning consent: ~66%
* Consent → financial close: ~75–85%


* **Overall:** roughly 8–15% of greenfield sites identified ever reach EPC signature.

| **Construction (~12 months)** | + £30–45m total capex | £600k–£900k | — |
| **COD** | Project operational | — | Acceptance certificates, taking-over certificate |

*For a 200 MW DCO project, multiply the dev-capital row at EPC signature by ~3–4x in absolute terms (£10–15m total at-risk dev capital), though the per-MW figure typically falls to £40,000–£70,000/MW owing to scale.*

## 12. Closing Observations

UK solar development in 2025/26 sits at an inflection point. The NSIP threshold increase to 100 MW, the TMO4+ "first ready, first connected" connection regime, the new 20-year CfD contracts, the record 4.9 GW awarded in AR7a at £65/MWh, and the Clean Power 2030 target of 45–57 GW together offer a structurally more favourable environment than at any point since the Renewables Obligation peak of 2014–16.

Nonetheless, the binding constraints have shifted: where once it was subsidy availability, today the principal gatekeepers are grid (specifically Gate 2 status and connection date), planning (specifically BMV land and LVIA), and offtake-vs-merchant economics. Cost discipline — module prices below £200,000/MW, EPC pricing of £550,000–£850,000/MW, total project capex below £1.1m/MW — is necessary but no longer sufficient.

The successful developer in 2026 is one who can sequence land, grid, planning, and offtake so that each gate clicks shut on time, allowing the project to reach EPC signature with around £60,000–£100,000/MW of equity at risk, before the larger £600,000+/MW EPC commitment is made. Roughly only one in eight greenfield options gets that far.

*Forecasts and forward-looking figures in this report (especially future strike prices, CPPA price ranges, market-size projections, Aurora/Cornwall Insight captured-price assumptions, and IndexBox's installed-capacity additions out to 2035) reflect industry consensus at time of writing and should be treated as scenario rather than as fixed values. Connection timetables under TMO4+, in particular, remain subject to NESO's evolving programme, with several already-announced delays since the original June 2025 go-live.*
